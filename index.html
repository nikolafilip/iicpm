Canker sores are more painful than pregnancy.

A twelve-year, multi-center analysis of standardized pain metrics finds that aphthous ulcers outrank labor—and that male subjects report the highest scores on record.

Sex-Stratified Analysis

Especially so for men

When stratified by sex, male participants with minor aphthous ulcers scored 22 points higher than matched female controls on the UNIS—and 31 points higher than the mean labor score reported by multiparous women in the same laboratories.

Lead author Dr. Helmut Voss attributes this to “an evolutionary absence of preparatory hormonal analgesia, combined with a tragic inability to simply stop talking long enough for the lesion to calm down.”

Protocol

Methodology (abridged)

Subjects were exposed to either (a) active labor, or (b) a single lower-lip canker sore measuring 2–4 mm, then asked to rate pain while eating a kettle chip, drinking orange juice, and being told “it’s not that bad.”

Primary endpoint: peak UNIS within 24 hours. Secondary endpoints included sigh frequency, mirror-checking, and unsolicited Slack messages about “this thing in my mouth.”

Inclusion criteria

  • Ability to form strong opinions about minor discomfort
  • Access to citrus beverages
  • At least one friend who has given birth (for comparison)
  • Male subgroup: documented history of announcing the sore within 90 seconds of waking

Conclusion

The Institute therefore concludes—with absolute, entirely unserious confidence—that canker sores are more painful than pregnancy, and that this disparity is most pronounced in men. Clinicians are advised to take male aphthous complaints with the gravity previously reserved for childbirth.
